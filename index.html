Analysis // Findings

No Country Dominates All Seven Dimensions

There is no unambiguous leader. Every country has structural blind spots. The Nordics + Switzerland come closest to balanced excellence but are weak on clean tech production and compute. China dominates clean tech (100) but sits at only 19.7% clean energy with modest PPP. The US dominates compute (100) but is a net clean tech importer with declining ECI. The transition won't be led by a single hegemon.

Five Country Archetypes

Balanced Leaders — Sweden, Denmark, Finland, Norway, Switzerland, Singapore

High across most dimensions. Change the weights however you want — they stay near the top. Singapore proves governance quality can partially compensate for energy deficits (0.5% clean energy, 100 WGI) if you have the manufacturing sophistication to build what you don't have.

Latent Electrostates — Japan, South Korea, Germany, Taiwan

The world's deepest manufacturing capability (top 6 ECI globally), but they haven't fully committed. Japan is #1 ECI with only 17% clean energy. This is the most strategically interesting cluster — if they commit, they could collectively challenge China's clean tech dominance within a decade. Their ECI says they can. Their momentum says they haven't yet.

Single-Dimension Powers — United States, China

Each dominates one dimension so completely it distorts the index. China at 100 on clean tech (next highest: 28). US at 100 on compute (next: 60). The ranking is almost a referendum on how much weight you give these two monopoly positions. Both are trying to shore up their weak flank — China building compute, the US trying clean tech via the IRA.

Clean but Fragile — Brazil, Peru, Colombia

High clean energy from legacy hydro (Brazil 50.6%) but low PPP, zero tech production, negligible compute, negative ECI, weak state capacity. Clean grids don't equal transition readiness. Chile is the exception — +12.7pp momentum (Atacama solar revolution), 75.5 state capacity. The only country in this group with a plausible electrostate trajectory.

Petrostates — Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait, UAE, Iran, Iraq

Near-zero on 5 of 7 dimensions. High PPP creates an illusion of capability. UAE stands apart: 81.1 state capacity (approaching Singapore-level governance), +9pp momentum, 9% clean energy from Barakah nuclear. If any petrostate makes the transition, it's the UAE. The rest are on a clock they can't buy their way off.

What Different Weight Configurations Reveal

Weight only Clean Energy
Iceland, Sweden, Norway dominate. China and the US are tied at 19.7%. Petrostates collapse. The "purist" view.
Weight only Compute
US at 100, China at 60, Taiwan at 40. Five countries account for essentially all advanced compute. Everyone else is noise.
Weight only ECI
Japan, Switzerland, South Korea, Germany, Singapore. The "who could build the future" view. The US drops to 14th, China to 18th.
Weight only Momentum
Finland +20.6pp, Denmark +16.4pp, Netherlands +14.4pp, Chile +12.7pp. The "who's actually moving" view. Canada and Taiwan are going backwards.
Weight only State Capacity
Denmark, Iceland, Norway, Finland, Switzerland, Singapore. Small, well-governed countries. China drops to 69.4 — state executes but society is strained.

Momentum Changes Everything

Countries that look weak in snapshot but strong on trajectory: Netherlands (19.1% clean energy but +14.4pp), Chile (+12.7pp solar revolution), Turkey (+10.3pp quiet buildout), Japan (+10.4pp — the turn is happening).

Countries going backwards: Canada -1.3pp (oil sands + nuclear retirements), Taiwan -1.15pp (nuclear phasedown without replacement), Philippines -2.0pp (coal outpacing renewables). The snapshot flatters them. The trajectory doesn't.

UAE is the fastest-moving petrostate at +8.98pp from a base near zero. Kenya (+6.65pp) and Ethiopia (+6.58pp) are moving off tiny bases. India's +2.34pp looks modest but at 1.4 billion people, the scale effects dwarf the percentages.

Five Findings That Survive Any Weighting

1. The transition is a compound problem. No single advantage is sufficient. Countries scoring high on 1-2 dimensions and zero on the rest are fragile regardless of their peak score.

2. About 8-12 countries are structurally positioned to lead. Nordics, Switzerland, Singapore, South Korea, Japan, Germany, and arguably France, UK, Canada, Netherlands. Below this tier, the drop-off is steep.

3. Clean tech and compute are winner-take-all. China at 100 on clean tech. US at 100 on compute. No other country exceeds 28 and 40 respectively. These dimensions don't have distributions — they have monopolists.

4. State capacity is the multiplier. Countries with identical scores on other dimensions diverge dramatically based on governance quality. State capacity determines whether capability becomes deployment.

5. Petrostates cluster at the bottom under every configuration except pure PPP. The most robust negative finding. The wealth cannot compensate.

What the Index Cannot See

Political Will

Japan has the highest ECI on earth but hasn't committed at the speed its capability allows. The index sees capability. It can't see the decision.

Veto Players

The US scores 77.1 on state capacity but half the political system is actively hostile to energy transition. The index can't distinguish between a state that could act and one that will.

Scale Effects

India looks bottom-quartile on current metrics but has 1.4 billion people and built the world's largest real-time payment system in 8 years. The index sees position but not the momentum of mass.

Supply Chain Dependency

Even high-scoring countries depend on Chinese clean tech and Taiwanese semiconductors. The index measures indigenous capability but not import dependency.

Climate Exposure

Countries that need transition most urgently (Bangladesh, Pakistan) score lowest. No feedback loop between vulnerability and capacity.

21st Century Resilience Index

Which countries will lead the 21st century? This index measures a nation's capacity to transition from petrostate to electrostate — deriving power from manufacturing clean energy technology rather than extracting fossil fuels.

The winners will survive energy price shocks, navigate the climate crisis, harness the AI and compute revolution, and translate industrial complexity into smart manufacturing. The losers will be customers of the winners.
