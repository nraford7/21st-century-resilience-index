Analysis // Findings

No Country Dominates All Nine Dimensions

There is no unambiguous leader. Every country has structural blind spots — and the three-axis structure makes this visible. X (Transition Readiness) measures what you have: clean energy, clean tech, compute & AI, and economic complexity. Y (Power to Execute) measures what you’ve proven: purchasing power, state capacity, and 20-year momentum. Z (Compound Exposure) measures what you face: climate vulnerability and resource insecurity. The Nordics + Switzerland come closest to balanced excellence but are weak on clean tech production and compute. China dominates clean tech (100) but sits at only 19.7% clean energy with modest PPP and high projected climate exposure (66.0). The US scores 97 on compute & AI (blended compute infrastructure + AI readiness) but is a net clean tech importer with declining ECI. Singapore’s perfect governance gets diluted by compound exposure — resource security of 12 and sea-level climate risk. The transition won’t be led by a single hegemon.

Six Country Archetypes

Balanced Leaders — Sweden, Denmark, Finland, Norway, Switzerland, Singapore*

High across most dimensions. Change the weights however you want — they stay near the top. Singapore proves governance quality can partially compensate for energy deficits (0.5% clean energy, 100 WGI) if you have the manufacturing sophistication to build what you don’t have. *Singapore’s position is now more nuanced — perfect governance but resource security of 12 and projected 2050 climate exposure of 56.5 (sea level rise). The governance compensates but the structural vulnerability is real.

Emerging Electrostates — Japan, South Korea, Germany, Taiwan

The world’s deepest manufacturing capability (top 6 ECI globally), but they haven’t fully committed. Japan is #1 ECI with only 17% clean energy. This is the most strategically interesting cluster — if they commit, they could collectively challenge China’s clean tech dominance within a decade. Their ECI says they can. Their 20-year momentum says they haven’t yet.

Single-Dimension Powers — United States, China

Each dominates one dimension so completely it distorts the index. China at 100 on clean tech (next highest: 28). US at 97 on compute & AI (next: 67). The ranking is almost a referendum on how much weight you give these two monopoly positions. Both are trying to shore up their weak flank — China building compute, the US trying clean tech via the IRA. The blended compute & AI score softens the US monopoly slightly — AI readiness is more distributed than raw compute infrastructure.

Clean but Fragile — Brazil, Peru, Colombia

High clean energy from legacy hydro (Brazil 50.6%) but low PPP, zero tech production, negligible compute, negative ECI, weak state capacity. Clean grids don’t equal transition readiness. Chile is the exception — 20yr momentum of 51.3 (Atacama solar revolution + GDP growth), 75.5 state capacity. The only country in this group with a plausible electrostate trajectory.

Petrostates — Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait, UAE, Iran, Iraq

Near-zero on most dimensions. High PPP creates an illusion of capability. UAE stands apart: 81.1 state capacity (approaching Singapore-level governance), 20yr momentum of 45.3, 9% clean energy from Barakah nuclear. If any petrostate makes the transition, it’s the UAE. The rest are on a clock they can’t buy their way off.

Crisis States — Philippines, Bangladesh, Pakistan, Nigeria

High projected 2050 climate exposure, low state capacity, low resource security. The countries most pressured to transition and least able to do so. Philippines (88.0 exposure, 52.9 state capacity), Bangladesh (91.5 exposure, 41.2 state capacity), Pakistan (84.0 exposure, 35.8 state capacity), Nigeria (78.5 exposure, 30.4 state capacity). Big dots in the bottom-left corner of the scatter = crisis.

The Resource Security Penalty

Resource security now lives on the Z axis (Compound Exposure), inverted — low resource security means higher exposure. Gulf states take a compound hit: already petrostate-trapped on the X axis, their dot size swells because they can’t feed or water themselves. Qatar — resource security of 8, climate 62.5 — has among the largest compound exposure dots on the chart. Kuwait at 9. UAE at 11. Singapore’s perfect governance score contrasts sharply with its compound exposure: resource security of 12 and sea-level climate risk of 56.5. Israel (28) faces similar structural vulnerability. The countries that buy everything are exposed to everything.

Compound Exposure as Urgency

The Z dimension (dot size in 2D, spatial axis in 3D) now combines climate vulnerability and resource insecurity into compound exposure. Bangladesh (climate 91.5, resource 41), Philippines (88.0, 52), India (87.5, 65), Pakistan (84.0, 50), Nigeria (78.5, 36) face the worst compound pressure. Gulf petrostates also swell — low climate scores compounded by near-zero resource self-sufficiency. The dot-size makes this visible: big dots in the bottom-left corner = crisis. Small dots in the upper-right corner = the countries that could help but face the least pressure to do so.

What Different Weight Configurations Reveal

Iceland, Sweden, Norway dominate. China and the US are tied at 19.7%. Petrostates collapse. The “purist” view.
Weight only Compute & AI
US at 97, China at 67, Taiwan at 55, South Korea at 48, Japan at 46. Blending compute infrastructure (60%) with AI readiness (40%) elevates countries with strong governance and research ecosystems. The raw compute monopoly softens but doesn’t disappear.

Japan, Switzerland, South Korea, Germany, Singapore. The “who could build the future” view. The US drops to 14th, China to 18th.
Weight only Resource Security
Brazil (92), US (89), Canada (88), Argentina (87), Australia (85) dominate. Gulf states collapse — Qatar 8, Kuwait 9, UAE 11. Singapore falls to near-bottom. Now on the Z axis (exposure), resource insecurity compounds climate vulnerability into bigger dots.

Switzerland (16.5), Iceland (17.5), Finland (18.5) lead. Bangladesh (91.5), Philippines (88.0), India (87.5) most penalized. The “who’s safest by 2050” view — uncorrelated with who’s richest.
Weight only 20yr Momentum

Weight only State Capacity
Denmark, Iceland, Norway, Finland, Switzerland, Singapore. Small, well-governed countries. China drops to 69.4 — state executes but society is strained.

Five Findings That Survive Any Weighting

1. The transition is a compound problem. No single advantage is sufficient. Countries scoring high on 1-2 dimensions and zero on the rest are fragile regardless of their peak score.

2. About 8-12 countries are structurally positioned to lead. Nordics, Switzerland, Singapore, South Korea, Japan, Germany, and arguably France, UK, Canada, Netherlands. Below this tier, the drop-off is steep.

3. Resource security compounds exposure on the Z axis. Resource insecurity now compounds with climate vulnerability into a single exposure measure. The Gulf states were already trapped on the X axis. Now their Z-axis compound exposure swells — they can’t feed or water themselves and face severe climate pressure. The dependency isn’t just on fossil fuel revenue; it’s on the entire global supply chain for survival basics.

4. Compound exposure creates urgency without capability. The countries most exposed to climate disruption and resource insecurity — Bangladesh, Philippines, India, Pakistan, Nigeria — are the least equipped to transition. The pressure is highest where the capacity is lowest. This is the structural injustice the index makes visible.

5. State capacity remains the multiplier. Countries with identical scores on other dimensions diverge dramatically based on governance quality. State capacity determines whether capability becomes deployment. Now amplified: state capacity also determines whether resource scarcity and climate exposure become crises or managed challenges.

What the Index Cannot See

Political Will

Japan has the highest ECI on earth but hasn’t committed at the speed its capability allows. The index sees capability. It can’t see the decision.

Veto Players

The US scores 77.1 on state capacity but half the political system is actively hostile to energy transition. The index can’t distinguish between a state that could act and one that will.

Scale Effects

India looks bottom-quartile on current metrics but has 1.4 billion people and built the world’s largest real-time payment system in 8 years. The index sees position but not the momentum of mass.

Supply Chain Dependency

Even high-scoring countries depend on Chinese clean tech and Taiwanese semiconductors. The index measures indigenous capability but not import dependency.

Beyond 2050 Climate Trajectories

The scores now project to 2050, but 2050-2100 trajectories could diverge further. Countries like Netherlands (44.0) and Singapore (56.5) already show significant projected exposure from sea level rise. Bangladesh’s 91.5 by 2050 could worsen dramatically under higher-emission scenarios beyond mid-century.

The 21st century belongs to electrostates.

Nations whose power comes from manufacturing clean energy technology — not extracting fossil fuels from the ground.

This index measures which countries are positioned to lead that transition, and which are trapped.

Only 8–12 countries are structurally positioned to lead.

The transition requires a compound of clean energy, manufacturing sophistication, economic complexity, compute capacity, governance quality, resource security, and momentum — simultaneously.

Most countries have one or two. Almost none have all of them.

The countries most exposed to climate change are the least prepared.

Bangladesh, Philippines, Pakistan, Nigeria — facing the worst projected climate impacts by 2050, with the lowest capacity to transition.

The gap between who needs the transition and who can execute it is the moral center of this index.

Wealth is not resilience. Position is not momentum.

Qatar has $129k GDP per capita and scores near the bottom. Japan has the world's deepest manufacturing capability and hasn't deployed it. Canada is going backwards on clean energy.

The index reveals what headlines miss: who's actually building the future, and who's coasting on the past.
