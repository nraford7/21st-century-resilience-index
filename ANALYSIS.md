# 21st Century Resilience Index: Analytical Findings

**85 nations. 9 dimensions. What the data reveals about who can execute the electrostate transition -- and who is decaying.**

---

## 1. No Country Dominates All Nine Dimensions

This is the headline finding. There is no unambiguous leader. Every country has structural blind spots.

The Nordics + Switzerland come closest to "balanced excellence" -- high clean energy, strong execution capacity, solid capital mobilization, and serious velocity. But they're weak on clean tech production (none are net exporters) and compute (no hyperscale presence). They're *resilient* but they're not *producing* the transition tools.

China is the only country in the top tier on clean tech (100), with the highest execution capacity score in the dataset (81), the highest transition velocity (+62), and massive capital mobilization (88). Its clean energy share is only 19.7% and its resource security is complicated (58.9 -- dominant in refining but dependent on imported raw materials). It's a production-and-execution powerhouse with a material dependency problem.

The US dominates compute (95) and has moderate capital mobilization (52), but its execution capacity has collapsed to 50 -- unable to permit a transmission line, unable to build high-speed rail, ASCE infrastructure grade C-. Its transition velocity is +2: near-stagnant, with infrastructure decay actively eroding renewable deployment gains. Its ECI ranking has been declining (13th to 20th over a decade). It's betting on one dimension while the built environment rots.

**Implication:** The transition won't be led by a single hegemon. It will be led by a small club of countries whose strengths compensate for each other's gaps -- or by whoever figures out how to score well across all nine dimensions first.

---

## 2. Five Country Archetypes

The data reveals five structural profiles:

### The Balanced Leaders
**Sweden, Denmark, Finland, Norway, Switzerland, Singapore**

High across most dimensions. Strong execution capacity, high capital mobilization, significant clean energy (except Singapore). What distinguishes them: *breadth*. Change the weights however you want -- they stay near the top. Their composite scores are robust to different assumptions.

Singapore is the outlier in this group: near-zero clean energy (0.5%) and the lowest resource & material security score in the top tier (10.6 -- imports everything), but the highest execution capacity on earth (92), elite capital mobilization (78 -- GIC + Temasek deploy $1.1T+), and high ECI (1.83). It proves that execution capacity can partially compensate for energy and material deficits -- but only if you have the financial architecture and manufacturing sophistication to build what you don't have.

### The Latent Electrostates
**Japan, South Korea, Germany, Taiwan**

Highest manufacturing complexity on earth (top 6 ECI globally), significant compute capacity, strong execution capacity (Japan 72, South Korea 80, Taiwan 76, Germany 63). But they haven't fully deployed their capabilities for the energy transition. Japan is at #1 ECI but only 17% clean energy. Germany is the only major Western net clean tech exporter but its nuclear exit dropped clean energy to 24%.

The material security problem is acute for this cluster. Japan (28.7), South Korea (26.2), and Taiwan (22.3) have near-total mineral import dependency -- the same structural vulnerability as petrostates, but for different inputs. The transition requires lithium, cobalt, rare earths, and copper at scale; these countries mine almost none of it. Their resource & material security scores dropped under the new framework that now captures this dependency.

**This is the most strategically interesting cluster.** If Japan, South Korea, and Germany fully commit and solve their materials problem, they could collectively challenge China's clean tech dominance within a decade. Their ECI scores say they have the capability. Their velocity scores (Japan +18, South Korea +14, Germany +20) say they're moving, but not fast enough.

### The Single-Dimension Powers
**United States, China**

Each dominates one dimension so completely that it distorts the entire index.

**China:** 100 on clean tech. No other country exceeds 28. Eighty percent of global solar manufacturing, 83% of batteries, 70% of EVs. But China now scores dominantly on *three* dimensions, not one: clean tech (100), execution capacity (81), and transition velocity (+62). It added more solar in 2024 than the rest of the world combined. It built 48,000 km of HSR in 15 years. It invested $890B in clean energy in a single year. The index is no longer a referendum on how much weight you give clean tech production -- China's position is structurally robust across execution, velocity, and capital mobilization (88). Its weak flanks are clean energy share (19.7%), resource & material security (58.9 -- refining dominance built on extraction dependency), and climate exposure.

**United States:** 95 on compute. The next highest is China at 71. Half of global hyperscale data center capacity. But the rebuild of the US profile under the new framework is the most dramatic change in the index. Execution capacity dropped from 77.1 (old state capacity) to 50 -- the gap between institutional quality on paper and the inability to build anything in practice. Transition velocity at +2 is near-zero, dragged down by a -12 infrastructure condition delta (bridges failing, grid aging, California HSR on year 16 with zero passengers). Capital mobilization (52) is moderate -- deep capital markets but no sovereign fund, no development bank, and political inability to deploy consistently.

The US is the only country in the top tier on *any* dimension that scores this poorly on execution. Wealth without delivery.

### The Clean-but-Fragile
**Brazil, Peru, Colombia, Chile (partially)**

High clean energy from hydropower -- Brazil at 50.6%, Peru at 26.7%, Colombia at 26.1%. But low capital mobilization, zero clean tech production, negligible compute, low ECI (all negative except Chile), and limited execution capacity (Brazil 47, Peru 37, Colombia 44).

This is the Mozambique Problem the index was designed to reveal. Clean energy from legacy hydro doesn't equal transition readiness. These countries can't manufacture the transition technologies, can't mobilize the capital to import them at scale, and don't have the execution capacity to deploy them systemically. They're clean but structurally fragile.

The resource & material security dimension now partially rescues some of these countries: Brazil (72.3 -- rare earths, graphite, niobium), Chile (64.5 -- copper and lithium giant), and Argentina (62.5 -- lithium triangle). They sit on the materials the electrostates need. Whether they can translate extraction into industrial capability is the question.

**Chile is the strongest exception:** Execution capacity at 56, +28 transition velocity (one of the fastest solar buildouts on earth, driven by Atacama), capital mobilization at 40 (largest EM sovereign green bond issuer), and massive mineral reserves (82 on critical minerals alone -- copper + lithium). Chile is the only country in this group with a plausible electrostate trajectory, and it may also become a critical node in the material supply chain.

### The Petrostates
**Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait, UAE, Iran, Iraq, Algeria**

Near-zero on clean energy (all below 1% except UAE at 9%). Moderate to high capital mobilization in some cases (UAE 70, Saudi 62 -- driven by sovereign fund assets) but near-zero on execution outside prestige projects. Zero clean tech. Zero compute. Low to negative ECI. Resource & material security is catastrophic: Kuwait (8.2), Qatar (7.6), UAE (12.5), Saudi (14.1) -- they import their food, their water, and their minerals.

The old PPP metric inflated these countries' Y-axis scores. Qatar at $129k PPP looked capable. Capital mobilization at 35 reveals the truth: the Kuwait Investment Authority has $920B in assets but invests $0 in domestic transition infrastructure. Wealth is not mobilization.

**UAE stands apart.** It scores 82 on execution capacity (Barakah nuclear in 12 years, Dubai built from desert, Etihad Rail), +28 transition velocity (strongest of any petrostate by far), and 70 on capital mobilization (ADIA + Mubadala + ADQ = $1.7T+ in deployable sovereign capital). If any petrostate makes the transition, it's the UAE. The rest are on a clock they can't buy their way off.

## 3. What Different Weight Configurations Reveal

The interactive weights are the most analytically powerful feature. Here's what different configurations expose:

### Weight only Clean Energy (w1=100, rest=0)
**Top 5:** Iceland (80.5), Sweden (72.2), Norway (71.8), Finland (63.2), Switzerland (56.8)

This is the "purist" view -- who has actually decarbonized their energy? The Nordic countries + hydro nations dominate. China and the US are tied at 19.7% -- identical despite radically different strategies. The petrostates collapse to the bottom.

### Weight only Compute (w3=100)
**Top 5:** US (95), China (71), Taiwan (64), South Korea (61), Japan (58)

The most concentrated dimension. Five countries account for essentially all advanced compute. This is where the leverage analysis from "Indispensable Fragility" becomes visible -- Taiwan's TSMC, South Korea's HBM, the Netherlands' ASML. Remove any one of these nodes and the global compute supply chain fragments.

### Weight only ECI (w4=100)
**Top 5:** Japan (2.26), Switzerland (2.14), South Korea (2.04), Germany (1.94), Singapore (1.83)

The "who could build the future if they decided to" view. Notably, the US drops to 14th (1.40) and China to 18th (1.33). The countries with the deepest manufacturing sophistication are not the same countries leading in clean energy or compute. The capability and the commitment are in different places.

### Weight only Transition Velocity (w7=100)
**Top 5:** China (+62), Denmark (+38), Finland (+35), Netherlands (+32), Uruguay (+30)

The "who's actually moving" view. Disrupts the snapshot completely. China's velocity is so far ahead of every other country that the gap is structural, not incremental. Uruguay at +30 represents the single largest renewable transformation in the dataset (61.3pp 20yr gain to 98% renewable electricity). Canada (-2) and the United States (+2) -- two of the wealthiest nations on earth -- are effectively stagnant or regressing.

### Weight only Execution Capacity (w5=100)
**Top 5:** Singapore (92), Denmark (83), UAE (82), China (81), Finland (81)

The delivery premium. This is the most disruptive reshuffle from the old framework. China jumps from 69.4 (old state capacity) to 81 -- rewarded for building 48,000 km of HSR, not penalized for authoritarianism. The US drops from 77.1 to 50 -- penalized for permitting paralysis, not rewarded for institutional quality on paper. Singapore (92) leads because it has near-perfect execution across all infrastructure dimensions.

### Weight only Capital Mobilization (w6=100)
**Top 5:** China (88), Singapore (78), South Korea (72), UAE (70), Germany (65)

This replaces the old PPP-only view. The reshuffling is dramatic. Qatar drops from $129k PPP (top 3 globally) to 35 on capital mobilization. Luxembourg drops from #1 PPP to 48. South Korea jumps from mid-pack PPP ($52k) to 72 -- KDB, NPS pension fund, and aggressive industrial investment. The metric now measures *deployable transition capital*, not household wealth.

### Equal weight on Transition Readiness only (w1,w2,w3,w4,w7 = 20 each, w5,w6 = 0)
**This isolates the X-axis:** Who has the technology and velocity?

China dominates on the strength of clean tech production (100), growing compute (71), and transition velocity (+62). The Nordics score well from clean energy and velocity. Japan and South Korea score well from ECI and compute. The US drops significantly -- high compute can't compensate for being a net clean tech importer with modest energy share and near-zero velocity.

### Equal weight on Power to Execute only (w5,w6 = 50 each, rest = 0)
**This isolates the Y-axis:** Can you build it and fund it?

Singapore, China, South Korea, Denmark, UAE top the chart. This is the starkest departure from the old framework. China at execution capacity 81 and capital mobilization 88 is now the highest-scoring country on the Y-axis. The US at execution capacity 50 and capital mobilization 52 is mid-table -- enormous potential constrained by inability to permit, build, and deploy consistently.

## 4. Transition Velocity Changes Everything

Velocity is the most disruptive dimension. It turns the snapshot into a trajectory -- and critically, it now allows *negative values*. Infrastructure decay is visible for the first time.

**Countries that look weak in snapshot but strong on velocity:**
- Netherlands: 19.1% clean energy (unremarkable) but +32 velocity -- the offshore wind revolution
- Chile: 32.3% clean energy but +28 velocity -- Atacama solar at extraordinary speed
- UAE: 9% clean energy but +28 velocity -- Barakah nuclear, massive solar from zero
- Uruguay: 50.6% clean energy but +30 velocity -- the largest renewable transformation in the dataset

**Countries with negative velocity -- the new insight:**
- Canada: -2. Going backwards. Nuclear retirements, oil sands expansion actively counteracting deployment. Infrastructure aging badly -- bridges, water systems, northern roads.
- United States: +2. Near-stagnant. Infrastructure condition delta is -12 (ASCE grade C-). Renewable deployment happens but infrastructure decay overwhelms it. Political oscillation on climate policy means gains under one administration get partially reversed under the next.
- Philippines: -4. Coal growth outpacing renewables. Actively regressing.
- Kuwait: -12. Zero clean deployment. Infrastructure stagnant. The purest petrostate inertia.
- Iraq: -18. Post-conflict devastation. Infrastructure in severe disrepair. The lowest velocity in the dataset.

**The US infrastructure decay story is the most consequential finding in the new framework.** The old Momentum metric gave the US +4.4pp (modest but positive). The new Transition Velocity reveals what the old metric hid: the US is simultaneously deploying renewables AND watching its built environment deteriorate. Bridges are failing. The grid is aging. High-speed rail doesn't exist. The net velocity is near zero because you can't build a 21st century energy system on a 20th century infrastructure that's falling apart.

**China's velocity is the benchmark.** At +62, it's nearly double the next-highest country (Denmark at +38). This isn't just renewable deployment -- it's 278 GW of solar in a single year, 48,000 km of HSR, UHV transmission grid, 35%+ EV sales share, all simultaneous. The velocity gap between China and the US (+62 vs +2) is the single most telling number in the entire index.

**Countries with high velocity relative to their starting point (the real stories):**
- UAE: +28 from a base of 0.07% -- the only petrostate with credible transition velocity
- Chile: +28 with solar buildout transforming the Atacama
- Israel: solar deployment + desalination model
- Kenya: +6 and Ethiopia +4 -- off a tiny base but geothermal and hydro moving
- Rwanda: +5 -- remarkable for income level, state execution outpacing much wealthier nations

## 5. Five Structural Findings That Survive Any Weighting Scheme

Regardless of how you set the weights, these patterns persist:

**1. The transition is a compound problem.** No single advantage is sufficient. You need some combination of energy, technology, capital, execution capacity, manufacturing sophistication, materials, and velocity. Countries that score high on 1-2 dimensions and zero on the rest are fragile no matter how impressive their peak score.

**2. About 8-12 countries are structurally positioned to lead.** The Nordics, Switzerland, Singapore, South Korea, Japan, Germany, China, and arguably France, Netherlands, and Australia. They have some combination of all nine ingredients. Below this top tier, the drop-off is steep.

**3. Clean tech production and compute are winner-take-all dimensions.** China at 100 on clean tech, the US at 95 on compute. No other country exceeds 28 and 71 respectively. These dimensions don't have a distribution -- they have a monopolist and everyone else. The index is highly sensitive to how much weight you give monopoly positions.

**4. Execution capacity is the multiplier.** Countries with identical scores on the other eight dimensions diverge dramatically based on ability to deliver. Singapore (92) and the US (50) have comparable capital mobilization and similar ECI profiles, but Singapore's execution capacity puts it in a different tier entirely. China (81) and India (54) share ambition and scale, but China's infrastructure delivery speed is the difference between aspiration and deployment. Execution capacity determines whether capability becomes reality.

**5. Petrostates cluster at the bottom under every configuration except pure capital mobilization (and even then, only partially).** This is the most robust negative finding. Weight any dimension other than sovereign fund assets and the petrostates -- Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait -- collapse to the bottom quartile. The old PPP metric gave them a false Y-axis position. Capital mobilization reveals that wealth sitting in a sovereign fund in London is not the same as capital deployed into transition infrastructure. UAE is the sole partial exception.

## 6. What the Index Cannot See

**Political will.** Japan has the highest ECI on earth but hasn't committed to the transition at the speed its capability allows. The index sees the capability. It can't see the decision.

**Veto players.** The US scores 50 on execution capacity, which now correctly penalizes permitting dysfunction and infrastructure decay. But the dimension still can't fully capture the fact that half the political system is actively hostile to energy transition. Execution capacity measures track record -- it can't predict whether the IRA survives a change of administration.

**Scale effects.** India at 10.3% clean energy, capital mobilization of 55, and execution capacity of 54 looks like a mid-table country. But it has 1.4 billion people, built the world's largest real-time payment system in 8 years, is adding renewable capacity at a rate that will reshape global energy markets, and has a transition velocity of +18 driven by massive infrastructure buildout. The index sees the current position but not the compounding potential of mass.

**Supply chain chokepoints.** The new Resource & Material Security dimension partially addresses this -- Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan now correctly show material import dependency (28.7, 26.2, 22.3). But the dimension still can't capture the fragility of *specific* chokepoints. Taiwan's TSMC produces 90% of advanced chips. ASML is the sole supplier of EUV lithography. A single blockade or natural disaster at these nodes breaks the entire transition supply chain. The index measures aggregate security, not single-point-of-failure risk.

**Climate exposure feedback loops.** The countries that need transition most urgently (Bangladesh, Pakistan, low-lying nations) score lowest. There's no feedback loop between vulnerability and capacity. The index measures who *can* transition, not who *must*. Bangladesh at -4 velocity and 25.8 material security is simultaneously the most climate-exposed and least transition-capable.

**Soft power and alliance effects.** Australia's resource & material security (84.5) is exceptional partly because of Quad partnerships, Five Eyes alignment, and bilateral mineral deals with Japan, the EU, and the US. The index captures the security outcome but not the diplomatic architecture that produces it. Countries with the same mineral reserves but worse alliances (DRC, Russia) score differently because access depends on geopolitics the index can't model.
