# 21st Century Resilience Index: Analytical Findings

**85 nations. 9 dimensions. What the data reveals about who will lead the electrostate transition — and who won't.**

---

## 1. No Country Dominates All Nine Dimensions

This is the headline finding. There is no unambiguous leader. Every country has structural blind spots.

The Nordics + Switzerland come closest to "balanced excellence" — high clean energy, high PPP, high state capacity, strong momentum. But they're weak on clean tech production (none are net exporters) and compute (no hyperscale presence). They're *resilient* but they're not *producing* the transition tools.

China is the only country in the top tier on clean tech (100) and scores well on compute (71), ECI (1.33), and momentum (+9.07pp). But its clean energy share is only 19.7%, its PPP is modest ($25k), and its state capacity (69.4) is held back by social stability concerns. It's a production powerhouse, not a balanced electrostate.

The US dominates compute (95) and has high PPP ($82k), but is a net clean tech *importer*, sits at only 19.7% clean energy, and its momentum (+4.4pp) is modest. Its ECI ranking has been declining (13th → 20th over a decade). It's betting on one dimension.

**Implication:** The transition won't be led by a single hegemon. It will be led by a small club of countries whose strengths compensate for each other's gaps — or by whoever figures out how to score well across all nine dimensions first.

---

## 2. Five Country Archetypes

The data reveals five structural profiles:

### The Balanced Leaders
**Sweden, Denmark, Finland, Norway, Switzerland, Singapore**

High across most dimensions. Strong governance, high PPP, significant clean energy (except Singapore). What distinguishes them: *breadth*. Change the weights however you want — they stay near the top. Their composite scores are robust to different assumptions.

Singapore is the outlier in this group: near-zero clean energy (0.5%) but perfect state execution (100 WGI), high ECI (1.83), and strong compute (13). It proves that governance quality can partially compensate for energy deficits — but only if you have the manufacturing sophistication to build what you don't have.

### The Latent Electrostates
**Japan, South Korea, Germany, Taiwan**

Highest manufacturing complexity on earth (top 6 ECI globally), significant compute capacity, strong state capacity. But they haven't fully deployed their capabilities for the energy transition. Japan is at #1 ECI but only 17% clean energy. Germany is the only major Western net clean tech exporter but its nuclear exit dropped clean energy to 24%.

These countries *could* lead the transition tomorrow. The industrial capability exists. What's missing is commitment — or in some cases, the political will to choose electrification over legacy energy systems. Japan's +10.4pp momentum suggests the turn is happening. Germany's +4.4pp suggests it's slower than it should be.

**This is the most strategically interesting cluster.** If Japan, South Korea, and Germany fully commit, they could collectively challenge China's clean tech dominance within a decade. Their ECI scores say they have the capability. Their momentum scores say they haven't yet.

### The Single-Dimension Powers
**United States, China**

Each dominates one dimension so completely that it distorts the entire index.

**China:** 100 on clean tech. No other country exceeds 28. Eighty percent of global solar manufacturing, 83% of batteries, 70% of EVs. The index is almost a referendum on how much weight you give clean tech production — weight it heavily and China leads the world; zero it out and China drops to mid-table.

**United States:** 95 on compute. The next highest is China at 71. Half of global hyperscale data center capacity. The same logic applies: weight compute heavily and the US leads; zero it and it drops.

The critical question for both: is single-dimension dominance durable? China is trying to build compute capacity. The US is trying to build clean tech (IRA). Both are trying to shore up their weak flank. The index snapshot can't tell you who will succeed — but it can tell you where the gap is.

### The Clean-but-Fragile
**Brazil, Peru, Colombia, Chile (partially)**

High clean energy from hydropower — Brazil at 50.6%, Peru at 26.7%, Colombia at 26.1%. But low PPP, zero clean tech production, negligible compute, low ECI (all negative), and weak state capacity (48-53).

This is the Mozambique Problem the index was designed to reveal. Clean energy from legacy hydro doesn't equal transition readiness. These countries can't manufacture the transition technologies, can't afford to import them at scale, and don't have the governance capacity to execute a systemic shift. They're clean but structurally fragile.

**Chile is the partial exception:** 75.5 state capacity, +12.73pp momentum (one of the fastest energy transitions on earth, driven by Atacama solar), and a functioning institutional framework. Chile is the only country in this group with a plausible electrostate trajectory.

### The Petrostates
**Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait, UAE, Iran, Iraq, Algeria**

Near-zero on clean energy (all below 1% except UAE at 9%). High PPP (Saudi $72k, Qatar $129k). Zero clean tech. Zero compute. Low to negative ECI.

The composite scores for this group range from 31 (Iraq) to 52 (UAE). The wealth creates an illusion of capability. But money alone can't buy transition readiness — you need manufacturing complexity, institutional capacity, and the industrial base to absorb and deploy new technology.

**UAE stands apart.** It scores 81.1 on state capacity (highest among petrostates by far), has +8.98pp momentum (also highest), and 9% clean energy (Barakah nuclear). Its ECI is modest (0.12) but its governance score (95.3 WGI) approaches Singapore levels. If any petrostate makes the transition, it's the UAE. The rest are on a clock they can't buy their way off.

## 3. What Different Weight Configurations Reveal

The interactive weights are the most analytically powerful feature. Here's what different configurations expose:

### Weight only Clean Energy (w1=100, rest=0)
**Top 5:** Iceland (80.5), Sweden (72.2), Norway (71.8), Finland (63.2), Switzerland (56.8)

This is the "purist" view — who has actually decarbonized their energy? The Nordic countries + hydro nations dominate. China and the US are tied at 19.7% — identical despite radically different strategies. The petrostates collapse to the bottom.

### Weight only Compute (w4=100)
**Top 5:** US (95), China (71), Taiwan (64), South Korea (61), Japan (58)

The most concentrated dimension. Five countries account for essentially all advanced compute. This is where the leverage analysis from "Indispensable Fragility" becomes visible — Taiwan's TSMC, South Korea's HBM, the Netherlands' ASML (16). Remove any one of these nodes and the global compute supply chain fragments.

### Weight only ECI (w5=100)
**Top 5:** Japan (2.26), Switzerland (2.14), South Korea (2.04), Germany (1.94), Singapore (1.83)

The "who could build the future if they decided to" view. Notably, the US drops to 14th (1.40) and China to 18th (1.33). The countries with the deepest manufacturing sophistication are not the same countries leading in clean energy or compute. The capability and the commitment are in different places.

### Weight only Momentum (w7=100)
**Top 5:** Finland (+20.6pp), Denmark (+16.4pp), Netherlands (+14.4pp), Chile (+12.7pp), Brazil (+11.9pp)

The "who's actually moving" view. Disrupts the snapshot completely. The Netherlands goes from mid-table (19.1% clean energy) to top 3. Japan jumps (+10.4pp). Canada and Taiwan — both positive on other dimensions — are going backwards.

### Weight only State Capacity (w6=100)
**Top 5:** Denmark (90.7), Iceland (90.6), Norway (90.3), Finland (90.1), Switzerland (89.7)

The governance premium. Small, well-run countries cluster at the top. Singapore (89.5) makes top 6. China drops to 69.4 — the state can execute (90 on adaptability) but social stability (46.3 FSI) and quality of life (67.6 SPI) drag it down.

### Equal weight on Transition Readiness only (w1,w3,w4,w5,w7 = 20 each, w2,w6 = 0)
**This isolates the X-axis:** Who has the technology?

China dominates on the strength of clean tech production (100) and growing compute (71). The Nordics score well from clean energy and momentum. Japan and South Korea score well from ECI and compute. The US drops significantly — high compute can't compensate for being a net clean tech importer with modest energy share and declining complexity.

### Equal weight on Power to Execute only (w2,w6 = 50 each, rest = 0)
**This isolates the Y-axis:** Can you afford it and govern it?

Singapore, Norway, Denmark, Switzerland, Iceland top the chart. These are wealthy and well-governed. The petrostates split: Qatar and UAE score well (high PPP + decent governance), while Iran, Iraq, and Nigeria collapse. China at 69.4 state capacity and $25k PPP sits in the middle — enormous execution capacity constrained by per-capita wealth and social fragility.

## 4. The Momentum Dimension Changes Everything

Momentum is the most disruptive dimension. It turns the snapshot into a trajectory.

**Countries that look weak in snapshot but strong on trajectory:**
- Netherlands: 19.1% clean energy (unremarkable) but +14.4pp in a decade (3rd fastest)
- Chile: 32.3% clean energy but +12.7pp — Atacama solar revolution
- Turkey: 19.8% clean energy but +10.3pp — quiet but significant
- Japan: 17% clean energy but +10.4pp — the turn is happening

**Countries that look strong in snapshot but are stalling or regressing:**
- Canada: 32.6% clean energy but -1.3pp — going backwards (nuclear retirements, oil sands)
- Taiwan: 8.6% clean energy and -1.15pp — nuclear phasedown without adequate replacement
- Philippines: 12.8% clean energy but -2.0pp — coal growth outpacing renewables
- Iceland: 80.5% but -2.5pp — ceiling effect, nowhere left to go

**Countries with high momentum relative to their starting point (the real stories):**
- UAE: +8.98pp from a base of 0.07% — the fastest-moving petrostate
- Israel: +8.81pp from 0.99% — solar buildout
- Indonesia: +6.25pp from 4.71% — starting from almost nothing
- Kenya: +6.65pp and Ethiopia +6.58pp — off a tiny base but moving

## 5. Five Structural Findings That Survive Any Weighting Scheme

Regardless of how you set the weights, these patterns persist:

**1. The transition is a compound problem.** No single advantage is sufficient. You need some combination of energy, technology, money, governance, manufacturing sophistication, and momentum. Countries that score high on 1-2 dimensions and zero on the rest are fragile no matter how impressive their peak score.

**2. About 8-12 countries are structurally positioned to lead.** The Nordics, Switzerland, Singapore, South Korea, Japan, Germany, and arguably France, UK, Canada, and the Netherlands. They have some combination of all nine ingredients. Below this top tier, the drop-off is steep.

**3. Clean tech production and compute are winner-take-all dimensions.** China at 100 on clean tech, the US at 95 on compute. No other country exceeds 28 and 71 respectively. These dimensions don't have a distribution — they have a monopolist and everyone else. The index is highly sensitive to how much weight you give monopoly positions.

**4. State capacity is the multiplier.** Countries with identical scores on the other eight dimensions diverge dramatically based on governance quality. Singapore and Hong Kong have similar PPP and compute profiles, but Singapore's governance score (100 vs ~70) puts it in a different tier entirely. State capacity determines whether capability becomes deployment.

**5. Petrostates cluster at the bottom under every configuration except pure PPP.** This is the most robust negative finding. Weight any dimension other than purchasing power and the petrostates — Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait — collapse to the bottom quartile. The wealth cannot compensate. UAE is the sole partial exception.

## 6. What the Index Cannot See

**Political will.** Japan has the highest ECI on earth but hasn't committed to the transition at the speed its capability allows. The index sees the capability. It can't see the decision.

**Veto players.** The US scores 77.1 on state capacity, which is reasonable for institutional quality. But half the political system is actively hostile to energy transition. The index can't distinguish between a state that *could* act and one that *will*.

**Scale effects.** India at 10.3% clean energy, $10k PPP, and 58.9 state capacity looks like a bottom-quartile country. But it has 1.4 billion people, built the world's largest real-time payment system in 8 years, and is adding renewable capacity at a rate that will reshape global energy markets. The index sees the current position but not the momentum of mass.

**Supply chain vulnerability.** Even high-scoring countries depend on Chinese clean tech manufacturing and Taiwanese semiconductors. The index measures indigenous capability but not import dependency.

**Climate exposure.** The countries that need transition most urgently (Bangladesh, Pakistan, low-lying nations) score lowest. There's no feedback loop between vulnerability and capacity. The index measures who *can* transition, not who *must*.
