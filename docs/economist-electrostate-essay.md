# The rise of the electrostate

*The war in Iran lays bare a structural shift. The 21st century will be shaped not by who has oil, but by who can turn cheap electrons into cheap intelligence*

---

The missiles arcing over the Strait of Hormuz carry an irony that few in the Gulf wish to contemplate. The war to protect oil infrastructure is accelerating the world's departure from oil. Every disrupted tanker, every spiking insurance premium, every barrel of crude priced to reflect the risk of Iranian retaliation makes the economics of electrification a little more compelling and the economics of extraction a little more fragile.

That shift has been building for years. But a new analytical framework — the 21st Century Resilience Index, which scores 85 nations across nine dimensions of structural readiness — puts numbers on the intuition. And those numbers, subjected to a chain analysis that few countries will find flattering, reveal something the simpler rankings obscured. The electrostate is not merely an energy proposition. It is a productivity revolution with four links, and most countries have broken at least one.

---

**the compound problem**

The index measures three clusters. Transition readiness: clean energy share, clean-tech manufacturing capacity, compute and AI infrastructure, and economic complexity. Power to execute: execution capacity, capital mobilisation and transition velocity. Compound exposure: climate vulnerability and resource-and-material security, now expanded to cover lithium, cobalt, rare earths and copper.

No country dominates all nine dimensions. The transition from petrostate to electrostate is a compound capability problem requiring technology, capital, minerals, governance and speed simultaneously. Roughly eight to twelve countries possess the structural prerequisites. Below that tier, the drop-off is steep.

---

**the intelligence dividend**

The original framing of the electrostate stopped at energy and infrastructure. That was insufficient. The full thesis runs through four links in a chain: cheap clean energy feeds cheap clean-tech manufacturing, which enables cheap compute, which produces cheap artificial intelligence applied across every sector of the economy — healthcare, agriculture, finance, governance, materials science, logistics. The endpoint is not decarbonisation. It is a productivity transformation of a kind not seen since electrification itself.

The Electrostate Dividend is the geometric mean of normalised scores across these four dimensions. The choice of geometric mean is deliberate: if any single link scores zero, the entire chain collapses to zero. A country with the world's best AI laboratories but no clean-energy base has not built a chain. It has built a node.

The scores reorder the world. China leads at 58 — the only country with a complete chain, from 278 gigawatts of solar added in 2024 through to dominant clean-tech manufacturing (score: 100), rapidly scaling compute infrastructure and the economic complexity to absorb AI across industrial sectors. Germany follows at 44, then South Korea and Denmark tied at 36, Japan at 34. These are not the rankings that the raw composite index produces. They are what emerges when you ask a harder question: which countries can convert electrons into intelligence at national scale?

The United States scores 11.

America's compute and AI score of 95 is the highest on earth. Half the world's hyperscale data centres sit on American soil. Its leading AI laboratories — in San Francisco, Seattle, New York — are without serious peer. But its clean-tech manufacturing score is effectively zero. It builds almost no solar panels, almost no batteries, almost no electric vehicles at volume. Its clean-energy share lags northern Europe by decades. The geometric mean is unforgiving: 95 multiplied by near-zero yields near-zero. The country with the best AI on earth has an Electrostate Dividend of 11 because the chain beneath the intelligence layer does not exist.

Singapore, the execution-capacity leader at 92, scores 10. Its clean-energy share is 0.5% — a city-state with no hinterland and no wind. The chain breaks at the first link. Qatar scores 3.

---

**the velocity gap**

The most telling number in the index is not a score but a difference. China's transition velocity is +62. America's is +2.

China added more solar capacity in 2024 than the rest of the world combined. It has built 48,000km of high-speed rail in fifteen years. It manufactures 80% of global solar panels, 83% of batteries and 70% of electric vehicles. Its execution-capacity score of 81 reflects the apparatus behind these outputs: policy banks, permitting measured in days rather than decades, state-directed capital deployment totalling $890bn in clean energy in a single year.

America scores 50 on execution capacity. The American Society of Civil Engineers grades national infrastructure at C-minus. Some 43% of roads are in poor or mediocre condition; 7.5% of bridges are structurally deficient. California's high-speed-rail project has been under construction for sixteen years without carrying a single passenger. The Inflation Reduction Act represents the largest climate investment in American history, and the semiconductor buildout under the CHIPS Act is real. Whether American political culture permits these investments to compound — or whether oscillation between administrations ensures each president partially reverses the last — is a question the index cannot answer. It measures track record, not will.

The chain analysis sharpens this picture. America's AI supremacy is genuine but suspended in air. It depends on Taiwanese semiconductors fabricated with equipment from the Netherlands, powered by a grid that still runs 60% on fossil fuels, in a country that manufactures virtually none of the hardware the energy transition requires. The chain is not merely incomplete. Its missing links are in other countries' hands.

---

**the latent electrostates**

The strategically interesting cluster sits in the middle of the Electrostate Dividend table: Germany at 44, South Korea at 36, Japan at 34. Add Denmark and Taiwan and you have the swing bloc of the transition.

These countries possess the world's deepest manufacturing capability. Japan leads the Harvard economic-complexity index. South Korea and Germany occupy the next positions. They have strong execution capacity and proven ability to build sophisticated things at scale. They have not deployed these capabilities for the energy transition at the speed their industrial base permits. Japan is the highest-scoring manufacturing economy ever built, with only 17% clean energy.

The chain analysis reveals their latent potential. Their manufacturing and complexity scores are high. Their clean-energy shares, while modest, are growing — Japan at +18 velocity, South Korea at +14, Germany at +20. Should these countries commit fully, their Electrostate Dividend scores could double within a decade. They are the nations most capable of completing the chain but least committed to doing so. Their structural position makes them the objects of intense courtship from both Washington and Beijing, neither of whom can complete the chain alone.

---

**the petrostate illusion**

The Gulf states illustrate the distinction between wealth and capability with painful clarity.

Qatar's GDP per capita exceeds $129,000. Its capital-mobilisation score is 35. The Qatar Investment Authority holds more than $500bn in assets and has invested approximately none of it in domestic electrostate infrastructure. Its material-security score is 7.6: it imports 90% of its food, desalinates virtually all its water and possesses zero critical-mineral reserves. Its Electrostate Dividend is 3.

Kuwait is worse. Its transition velocity is -12, meaning it is actively regressing. Saudi Arabia, for all the ambition of Vision 2030, scores 55 on execution capacity. It can build prestige megaprojects. It has not demonstrated the capacity to build transmission grids, manufacturing supply chains and water systems at the scale the electrostate requires.

The sole exception is the UAE. Execution capacity of 82, $1.7trn in deployable sovereign capital, transition velocity of +28 and a 9% clean-energy share from nuclear that exceeds the petrostate threshold. If any Gulf state makes the transition, it will be this one.

---

**a new resource curse**

The electrostate is not resource-independent. It is differently resource-dependent.

Every solar panel requires silicon and silver. Every battery requires lithium, cobalt and nickel. Every EV needs roughly four times as much copper as a combustion vehicle. The IEA estimates that net-zero by 2050 demands a sixfold increase in critical-mineral inputs. These minerals are concentrated in ways that mirror — and in some cases exceed — the concentration of oil. The DRC produces 51% of the world's cobalt. Chile holds 28% of copper reserves. China refines between 60% and 80% of virtually every critical mineral.

Australia, food-secure, water-secure and sitting on the world's largest lithium reserves, jumps to 84.5 on resource-and-material security. Japan drops to 24.1 — near-total mineral-import dependency, structurally analogous to petrostate vulnerability but for different inputs. South Korea falls to 25.3. These are the world's most capable manufacturers. They cannot source the materials for what they need to build.

China's position is characteristically paradoxical. It dominates mineral processing but imports the raw ores, principally from the DRC, Indonesia and Australia. It is the OPEC of refining, built on an extraction dependency it does not control. Should any primary supplier redirect flows — as Australia is doing through the Quad partnership and bilateral deals with Japan and the EU — the refining monopoly becomes a bottleneck rather than an advantage.

---

**what the numbers miss**

No index captures political will. No index captures scale dynamics: India at 10.3% clean energy looks mid-table, but 1.4bn people adding renewable capacity at even modest rates will reshape global energy markets within a decade. And no index models single points of failure. Taiwan's TSMC produces 90% of advanced semiconductors. A single incident in the Taiwan Strait would break the compute link of the electrostate chain for every country on earth.

What the Electrostate Dividend does capture is the difference between having a capability and having a chain. The United States has the world's most advanced AI. It does not have an electrostate. China has the only complete chain. It does not have the minerals to sustain it independently. The latent electrostates of north-east Asia and northern Europe have the manufacturing depth to build anything. They have not yet chosen to build this.

The war in Iran will end. The structural shift it has accelerated will not reverse. The century ahead belongs not to the nations with the most impressive single capability but to those that complete the chain — from electron to factory to server to applied intelligence — and do so using materials they can secure. The rest will discover what the petrostates are discovering now: that excellence in one dimension, however spectacular, is not a substitute for the whole.
